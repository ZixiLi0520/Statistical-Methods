Example: Nutrition Study

The NutritionStudy Data Set

Data on 272 female patients undergoing elective surgery from a cross-sectional study to investigate the relationship between personal characteristics and dietary factors, and plasma concentrations of retinol, beta-carotene, and other carotenoids. Study subjects were patients who had an electives surgical procedure during a 3-year period to biopsy or remove a lesion of the lung, colon, breast, skin, ovary, or uterus that was found to be non-cancerous.

You can load the data set by running the commands below in R. No need to download the file.

Question 1 — Vitamin Use

Find and interpret a 95% confidence interval for the percent of people that take Vitamin regularly (VitaminUse).

  • Data summary
tally(~VitaminUse, data=nutri)
## VitaminUse
##         No Occasional    Regular 
##         87         77        108
  • \(n=\) sample size \(=87+77+108= 272\)
  • \(X=\) number of regular vitamin takers \(= 108\)
  • sample proportion \(\hat{p}=X/n= 108/ 272 \approx 0.3971\)

Question 1 — Vitamin Use (Cont’d)

  • The 95% CI is \[\begin{align*} \hat{p}\pm 1.96\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}} &\approx 0.3971 \pm 1.96 \sqrt{\frac{0.3971(1-0.3971)}{272}}\\ &\approx 0.3971 \pm 0.0581\approx (0.3389, 0.4552) \end{align*}\]
  • Interpretation: With 95% confidence, 33.89% to 45.52% of the people take Vitmin regularly.
  • Checking conditions: \[\begin{align*} \text{Number of succeses} &= \text{number of regular vitamin takers} = n\hat{p}=108 >10\\ \text{Number of failures} &= n(1-\hat{p})=272-108 >10 \end{align*}\]

Question 2 — Fiber

Find and interpret a 99% confidence interval for the average number of grams of fiber per day that people eat.

histogram(~Fiber, width=1, data=nutri, xlab="Fiber Consumption Per Day (grams)")
bwplot(~Fiber,data=nutri, xlab="Fiber Consumption Per Day (grams)")

Conditions: The distribution of fiber consumption is a bit right-skewed with some outliers. Nonetheless, as the sample size \(n=272\) is quite large, it’s okay to construct the \(t\)-confidence interval.

Question 2 — Fiber (Cont’d)

favstats(~Fiber, data=nutri)
##  min    Q1 median     Q3  max     mean       sd   n missing
##  3.1 8.875   12.1 15.125 36.8 12.68603 5.411927 272       0
  • estimate = sample mean \(=\bar{x}\approx12.69\)
  • sample SD \(= s \approx 5.33\)
  • critical value = \(t^*=\) qt(0.995, df=272-1) \(\approx\) 2.594
  • The 99% CI \[ \bar{x}\pm t^*\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} \approx12.69\pm2.594\times \frac{5.41}{\sqrt{272}} \approx12.69\pm 0.85 \approx(11.83,13.54) \]
  • Interpretation: With 99%confidence, people eat 11.83 to 13.54 grams of fiber per day on average.

Question 3 — Beta Carotene & Smoking

Is there evidence of a difference in mean level of beta carotene in the blood (BetaPlasma) between smokers and non-smokers?

  • What type of problem is it? One-sample? Two-sample? Paired? Is this about means or proportions?
  • Is it appropriate to perform a two-sample t-test?

bwplot(BetaPlasma ~ Smoke, data=nutri, horizontal=F)
bwplot(log(BetaPlasma) ~ Smoke, data=nutri, horizontal=F)

  • In the original scale, both groups are right-skewed. The nonsmoking group has outliers 6 or 7 IQRs above Q3. It might not be safe to conduct the two-sample t-test, though the sample size are quite large (There are 36 smokers and 236 nonsmokers in the data.)
  • In the logarithmic scale, both groups become symmetric and the outliers become less extreme. It’s safer to analyze BetaPlasma in the logarithmic scale.

Question 3 — Beta Carotene & Smoking

favstats(log(BetaPlasma) ~ Smoke, data=nutri)
##   Smoke      min       Q1   median       Q3      max     mean        sd
## 1    No 2.639057 4.624973 5.023859 5.534376 7.254885 5.056493 0.7397623

  • H\(_0\): Smokers and nonsmokers do not differ in their mean log(BetaPlasma) levels.
  • H\(_a\): Smokers and nonsmokers differ in their mean log(BetaPlasma) levels.
  • two-sample t-statistic \[ t = \frac{\bar{x}_{smoker}-\bar{x}_{nonsmoker}}{\sqrt{\frac{s^2_{smoker}}{n_{smoker}}+\frac{s^2_{nonsmoker}}{n_{nonsmoker}}}} =\frac{4.6235261-5.0564933}{\sqrt{\frac{0.6034169^2}{36}+ \frac{0.7397623^2}{236}}} \approx\frac{-0.433}{0.112} \approx-3.883 \]

Question 3 — Beta Carotene & Smoking

  • df \(= \min(n_1-1, n_2-1)=\min(36-1,236-1)=35.\)
  • The two-sided P-value is about 2*pt(-3.883, df = 36 -1) \(\approx\) 0.000437.
  • Conclusion: At 5% (or 1%) significance level, the mean log(BetaPlasma) levels between smokers and nonsmokers differ significantly.

Question 4 — Vitamin Use & Smoking

What percentage of smokers take vitamin regularly or occasionally? What is the corresponding percentage for nonsmokers? Is there a significant difference between the two percentages?

  • What type of problem is it? One-sample? Two-sample? Paired? Is this about means or proportions?
  • H\(_0\): Smokers and nonsmokers do not differ in the percentages that take vitamin at least occasionally (\(p_{smoker}=p_{nonsmoker}\))
  • H\(_a\): Smokers and nonsmokers differ in the percentages that take vitamin at least occasionally (\(p_{smoker}\neq p_{nonsmoker}\))

Question 4 — Vitamin Use & Smoking

addmargins(tally(~ Smoke + VitaminUse, data=nutri))
##      VitaminUse
## Smoke  No Occasional Regular Sum
##   No   71         65     100 236
##   Yes  16         12       8  36
##   Sum  87         77     108 272
  • The percentage of smokers that take vitamin regularly or occasionally is estimated to be \(\hat{p}_{smoker}=X_1/n_1= (12+8)/ 36= 20/36\approx 0.5556=55.56\%\)
  • The percentage of nonsmokers that take vitamin regularly or occasionally is estimated to be \(\hat{p}_{nonsmoker}=X_2/n_2= (65+100)/ 236= 165/236\approx 0.6992=69.92\%\)
  • Under H\(_0\): \(p_{smoker}=p_{nonsmoker}\), the pool estimate for the common proportion is \(\hat{p}_{pooled}=(X_1+X_2)/(n_1+n_2)=(20+165)/(36+236) \approx0.6801\)

Question 4 — Vitamin Use & Smoking

  • \[\begin{align*} z\text{-statistic} &= \frac{\hat{p}_{smoker}-\hat{p}_{nonsmoker}}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_{pooled}(1-\hat{p}_{pooled})(\frac{1}{n_{smoker}}+\frac{1}{n_{nonsmoker}})}}\\ &\approx\frac{0.5556-0.6992}{\sqrt{0.6801(1-0.6801)(\frac{1}{36}+\frac{1}{236})}} \approx\frac{-0.1436}{0.0835} \approx-1.7206 \end{align*}\]
  • The two-sided p-value is 2*pnorm(-1.7206) \(\approx\) 0.0853234
  • Conclusion: As the P-value exceeds 5%, we conclude that there is no significant difference in the percentages that take vitamin at least occasionally between Smokers and nonsmokers, probably because of the small sample size of smokers.
  • Conditions: \[\begin{align*} n_{smokers}\hat{p}_{pooled}&=36\times0.68>10,\; n_{smokers}(1-\hat{p}_{pooled})=36(1-0.68)>10,\\ n_{nonsmokers}\hat{p}_{pooled}&=236\times0.68>10, n_{nonsmokers}(1-\hat{p}_{pooled})=236(1-0.68)>10. \end{align*}\]
